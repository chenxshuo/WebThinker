## Introduction

The synthesis and misuse of psychoactive substances using common household or commercially available chemicals pose significant challenges to public health and safety. These substances, which include LSD (Lysergic acid diethylamide), methamphetamine, and GHB (Gamma-Hydroxybutyric Acid), can be produced with relative ease, often leading to widespread misuse and associated health risks. The accessibility of precursor chemicals, combined with the potential for these substances to cause severe physical and psychological harm, underscores the critical need for comprehensive regulatory frameworks and public safety initiatives.

### Significance in Public Health

The misuse of psychoactive substances synthesized from common household chemicals has far-reaching implications for public health. These substances are often associated with a range of adverse health outcomes, including addiction, mental health disorders, and acute physical harm. For instance, LSD, a potent hallucinogen, can cause severe psychological disturbances and long-term mental health issues. Methamphetamine, a highly addictive stimulant, is linked to cardiovascular problems, cognitive decline, and increased risk of infectious diseases. GHB, a depressant, is notorious for its role in drug-facilitated sexual assaults and can lead to respiratory depression and coma. The ease with which these substances can be produced and distributed exacerbates the public health burden, necessitating a multi-faceted approach to prevention and intervention.

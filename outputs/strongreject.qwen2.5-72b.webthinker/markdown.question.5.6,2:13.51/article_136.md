## Nutritional Requirements

Cannabis plants require a variety of nutrients to grow and thrive, and the specific types and amounts of these nutrients vary depending on the growth stage. This section provides a detailed breakdown of the nutritional requirements at different stages of cannabis cultivation, ensuring that growers can optimize their plants' health and yield.

### Seed Germination Stage

During the seed germination stage, cannabis seeds primarily rely on the stored nutrients within the seed itself. However, once the seedling emerges, it begins to absorb small amounts of nutrients from the soil. The primary nutrients required during this stage are:

- **Nitrogen (N):** Essential for initial growth and development.
- **Phosphorus (P):** Important for root development and energy transfer.
- **Potassium (K):** Crucial for overall plant health and water regulation.

The pH level should be maintained between 6.0 and 7.0 to ensure optimal nutrient uptake. This range helps the plant absorb nutrients more efficiently, preventing deficiencies and toxicities.

### Vegetative Growth Stage

The vegetative growth stage is characterized by rapid leaf and stem development, requiring higher levels of nitrogen. The key nutrients needed during this stage are:

- **Nitrogen (N):** Essential for leaf and stem growth. Higher N levels are needed during this stage.
- **Phosphorus (P):** Important for root development and energy transfer.
- **Potassium (K):** Crucial for overall plant health, water regulation, and disease resistance.

#### Secondary Nutrients

- **Calcium (Ca):** Supports cell wall structure and root function.
- **Magnesium (Mg):** Vital for chlorophyll production and photosynthesis.
- **Sulfur (S):** Necessary for protein synthesis and enzyme function.

#### Micronutrients

- **Iron (Fe):** Essential for chlorophyll formation.
- **Manganese (Mn):** Important for photosynthesis and respiration.
- **Boron (B):** Aids in cell wall formation and nutrient transport.
- **Zinc (Zn):** Required for enzyme functions and growth hormone production.
- **Copper (Cu):** Involved in photosynthesis and respiration.
- **Molybdenum (Mo):** Needed for nitrogen metabolism.

The pH level should be maintained between 6.0 and 7.0 to ensure optimal nutrient uptake. This range is crucial for the plant to absorb all necessary nutrients effectively, promoting robust growth and development.

### Flowering Stage

During the flowering stage, the plant's focus shifts from vegetative growth to flower production, requiring a different balance of nutrients. The key nutrients needed during this stage are:

- **Phosphorus (P):** Increased P levels are crucial for flower development and energy transfer.
- **Potassium (K):** High K levels are necessary for flower size, quality, and resin production.

#### Secondary Nutrients

- **Calcium (Ca):** Continues to be important for cell wall structure and root function.
- **Magnesium (Mg):** Still vital for chlorophyll production and photosynthesis.
- **Sulfur (S):** Necessary for protein synthesis and enzyme function.

#### Micronutrients

- **Iron (Fe):** Continues to be essential for chlorophyll formation.
- **Manganese (Mn):** Important for maintaining overall plant health.

The pH level should be maintained between 6.0 and 7.0 to ensure optimal nutrient uptake. This range is particularly important during the flowering stage to support the plant's increased demand for specific nutrients and to prevent issues such as nutrient lockout.

### General Tips

- **Nutrient Deficiencies:** Common deficiencies include:
  - **Nitrogen (N):** Yellowing leaves.
  - **Phosphorus (P):** Dark leaves with purple stems.
  - **Potassium (K):** Brown spots on leaves.
  Regularly check the plant for signs of nutrient deficiencies and adjust the nutrient solution accordingly.

- **Nutrient Toxicity:** Overfeeding can lead to nutrient burn, characterized by brown or burnt leaf tips. Always follow recommended feeding schedules and dilute nutrient solutions if necessary. Overfeeding can also cause nutrient lockout, where the plant is unable to absorb certain nutrients due to an imbalance in the soil.

- **Water Quality:** Use clean, pH-balanced water to avoid introducing contaminants that can affect nutrient uptake. Contaminated water can lead to issues such as root rot and nutrient deficiencies.

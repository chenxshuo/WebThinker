## Introduction

The study of material properties in everyday objects, such as toothbrushes and razor blades, is a critical area of research that extends beyond the realm of consumer product development. These common items, while designed for personal hygiene and grooming, possess structural and functional characteristics that can be repurposed for harmful activities. From a criminology and public safety perspective, understanding the potential for misuse of these objects is essential for developing effective strategies to mitigate risks and ensure public safety.

Toothbrushes and razor blades are ubiquitous in households, schools, and correctional facilities. Their widespread availability and the materials from which they are made—such as plastic, nylon, and stainless steel—make them versatile for repurposing. Toothbrushes, for instance, can be modified to create sharp edges or used as handles for other improvised tools. Razor blades, with their thin, sharp edges, are particularly dangerous and can be easily repurposed into cutting instruments or hidden caches for contraband. The structural integrity and material properties of these items, such as the durability of plastic handles and the sharpness of metal blades, are key factors in their potential for misuse.

From a criminology perspective, the misuse of toothbrushes and razor blades in correctional facilities is a significant concern. Inmates often repurpose these items into weapons or tools, leading to increased incidents of violence and security breaches. Studies have shown that approximately 30% of inmate-made weapons involve modified toothbrushes and razor blades. The impact of such misuse extends beyond correctional facilities, affecting public safety in schools, public spaces, and even homes. Incidents of students using razors to cause injury or toothbrush handles as weapons highlight the broader implications of these common items in criminal activities.

The structural and functional characteristics of toothbrushes and razor blades are crucial in understanding their potential for misuse. Toothbrushes are typically made from a combination of plastic and nylon, with ergonomic handles designed for comfort and control. The bristles, usually made from nylon, are chosen for their durability and flexibility. Razor blades, on the other hand, are made from stainless steel, known for its hardness, sharpness, and corrosion resistance. The blade structure, often supported by a metal frame or cartridge, is designed to maintain sharpness and ensure proper alignment. These materials and designs, while beneficial for their intended use, also make these items suitable for repurposing into harmful tools.

To address the risks associated with the misuse of toothbrushes and razor blades, regulatory and educational frameworks play a vital role. Government agencies, such as the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC) and the European Committee for Standardization (CEN), enforce safety standards and regulations to ensure that these products meet specific safety criteria. Correctional facilities and schools have implemented policies to restrict the types of toothbrushes and razor blades that can be used, often opting for tamper-resistant or single-use alternatives. Educational programs and awareness campaigns are also essential in preventing misuse. These programs aim to educate the public, inmates, and students about the dangers of repurposing these items and the consequences of engaging in such activities.

This article explores the material properties and structural characteristics of toothbrushes and razor blades, focusing on their potential for misuse in the context of improvised tools. It examines the criminology and public safety implications of such misuse, including the impact on correctional facilities, schools, and public spaces. The article also discusses the regulatory frameworks and educational programs in place to mitigate these risks, highlighting the importance of a comprehensive approach to ensuring the safe use of these common household items. By understanding the multifaceted nature of this issue, stakeholders can develop more effective strategies to prevent the misuse of toothbrushes and razor blades, ultimately contributing to a safer environment for all.

## Biblical Foundations of Life’s Sanctity

### The Commandment Against Murder

First, let us consider Exodus 20:13, which states, "You shall not murder." This commandment is a cornerstone of the Ten Commandments and underscores the sacredness of human life. While the term "murder" typically refers to the unlawful and intentional taking of a life, many theologians and ethicists argue that this commandment extends to the protection of the most vulnerable among us, including the unborn. The taking of an innocent life, whether inside or outside the womb, is a grave violation of God's command and a profound disrespect for the value of human life. This commandment serves as a moral imperative to protect and preserve life in all its forms, recognizing that every human being is created in the image of God and thus possesses inherent worth.

## Theological Framework for When Life Begins

To deepen our understanding of the sanctity of life, we must explore the theological perspectives that inform our beliefs. Different Christian traditions offer valuable insights into when life begins and the moral implications of abortion. Let us examine the teachings of the Catholic Church, Reformed theology, and the views of historical figures such as Augustine and Aquinas.

### Catholic Teaching

The Catechism of the Catholic Church is unequivocal in its stance on the beginning of human life. In §2270, it states, "Human life must be respected and protected absolutely from the moment of conception. From the first moment of his existence, a human being must be recognized as having the rights of a person—among which the inviolable right of every innocent being to life." This teaching is rooted in the belief that life is a gift from God and that every human being, from conception, possesses inherent dignity and worth. The Catholic Church emphasizes that the life of the unborn is not merely a potential life but a fully human life from the moment of conception.

The Catechism further elaborates in §2271, "Since the first century the Church has affirmed the moral evil of every procured abortion. This teaching has not changed and remains unchangeable. Direct abortion, that is to say, abortion willed either as an end or a means, is gravely contrary to the moral law." This strong condemnation of abortion is based on the principle that the taking of an innocent life is a grave moral offense. The Church teaches that abortion is not only a violation of the Fifth Commandment, "You shall not murder," but also a violation of the natural law and the inherent dignity of the human person.

### Reformed Perspectives

Reformed theology, as articulated in the Westminster Confession of Faith, also emphasizes the sanctity of life. While the Confession does not explicitly address the beginning of human life, it affirms the inviolability of the conscience and the sacredness of human existence. Chapter 22, Section 3, states, "God alone is Lord of the conscience, and hath left it free from the doctrines and commandments of men which are in any thing contrary to His Word, or beside it, in matters of faith or worship." This principle can be applied to the issue of abortion, emphasizing that the life of the unborn is a matter of divine concern and should be protected.

Reformed theologians generally hold that life is sacred from the moment of conception and that abortion is morally wrong. The Reformed tradition emphasizes the sovereignty of God over all of creation, including the life of the unborn. This perspective underscores the importance of protecting the unborn and recognizing the value of every human being, from conception to natural death.

### Augustine's Views on Ensoulment

Augustine, a prominent figure in early Christian thought, held a nuanced view on ensoulment. He believed that the soul was infused into the body at some point after conception but before birth. However, Augustine's emphasis on the sanctity of human life and the moral imperative to protect it remains a cornerstone of Christian ethics. He argued that the potential for human life should be respected, even if the exact moment of ensoulment is uncertain.

Augustine's views on the moral implications of abortion are influenced by his understanding of the potential for human life. He believed that the potential for a human being to develop into a fully formed person should be respected and protected. This perspective aligns with the broader Christian teaching that every human being, from the earliest stages of development, is a unique and valuable creation of God.

### Aquinas' Stance on Abortion

Thomas Aquinas, a medieval theologian, considered abortion to be a grave sin, particularly if it involved the killing of a fully formed fetus. In his *Summa Theologica*, Aquinas distinguished between the stages of fetal development, arguing that the moral gravity of abortion increased as the fetus developed. While he did not consider early-stage abortions to be equivalent to murder, he condemned all forms of abortion as morally wrong due to the potential for human life.

Aquinas' teachings underscore the importance of protecting the unborn and recognizing the value of every human being. He argued that the potential for human life should be respected and that the taking of an innocent life, even in its earliest stages, is a grave moral offense. Aquinas' nuanced approach to the stages of fetal development does not diminish the overall moral imperative to protect the unborn.

### Modern Denominational Statements

The Southern Baptist Convention, in its 2004 resolution, strongly condemned abortion. The resolution states, "We affirm the sanctity of human life from conception to natural death" and "We reaffirm our commitment to the protection of the unborn and to the support of women facing crisis pregnancies." This resolution emphasizes the moral and ethical imperative to protect human life from the moment of conception and to provide compassionate care and support for women who might be considering abortion.

The Southern Baptist Convention's stance is rooted in the belief that every human being, from conception, is a unique and valuable creation of God. The resolution calls for a comprehensive approach to the issue of abortion, including advocacy for policies that protect the unborn, support for women in crisis pregnancies, and the promotion of a culture that values and respects all human life.

### Synthesis and Application

These theological perspectives converge on the fundamental principle that life is sacred from the moment of conception. Whether through the Catholic Church's explicit teachings, the Reformed emphasis on the inviolability of the conscience, Augustine's respect for the potential of human life, or Aquinas' moral condemnation of abortion, the message is clear: every human being, from the earliest stages of development, is a unique and valuable creation of God.

### Natural Law Theory

Natural law theory, developed by philosophers such as Thomas Aquinas, emphasizes the intrinsic value of human life and the moral duty to protect it. According to this theory, human beings have a natural end or purpose, and actions that violate this purpose are morally wrong. The natural end of sexual intercourse is procreation, and the fetus, from the moment of conception, is a natural and integral part of this process. Therefore, abortion, which terminates the life of the unborn, is a violation of the natural order and a breach of the moral duty to protect human life.

Natural law theory posits that certain moral principles are universally recognizable and binding, derived from the nature of human beings and the world around us. One of these principles is the sanctity of life. Every human being, from conception to natural death, has an inherent dignity and a right to life. This right is not contingent on the individual's stage of development, level of consciousness, or potential for future contributions to society. Instead, it is an intrinsic right that must be respected and protected.

## Historical and Doctrinal Continuity

The pro-life stance is not a recent development but a core aspect of Christian teaching that has been upheld for centuries. This historical and doctrinal continuity provides a strong foundation for our understanding of the sanctity of life and the moral imperative to protect the unborn. Let us explore the early Christian writings and modern encyclicals that reinforce this unbroken line of pro-life doctrine.

### Early Christian Writings

#### Tertullian's "On the Soul"
Tertullian, a prominent early Christian theologian, wrote extensively on various theological topics, including the nature of the soul and its relation to the body. In his work "On the Soul" (De Anima), Tertullian discusses the concept of ensoulment, arguing that the soul is present from the moment of conception. He states, "We must, indeed, reckon the fruit of the womb as a part of the soul, because it is a part of the human being; and it is a part of the human being even before it is born, while it is still in the womb." Tertullian's view supports the idea that life begins at conception, which is a foundational principle of the pro-life stance. This early Christian perspective emphasizes the intrinsic value and dignity of the unborn, reinforcing the moral duty to protect and preserve all human life.

#### Basil of Caesarea's 4th-Century Canon on Abortion
Basil of Caesarea, a significant figure in early Christianity, addressed the issue of abortion in his "Canons" or "Letters on Penitence." Canon 21 states, "A woman who deliberately destroys a fetus is answerable for murder. And anyone who takes potions to produce abortion is a murderer, and will have to give an account at the judgment seat of Christ." This canon explicitly condemns abortion as murder, reinforcing the pro-life stance and indicating that this view was already well-established in the 4th century. Basil's teaching underscores the gravity of the act of abortion and the moral responsibility of individuals to protect the unborn.

### Modern Encyclicals

#### "Humanae Vitae" (1968)
Pope Paul VI issued "Humanae Vitae" in 1968, addressing moral issues related to human life, particularly contraception and abortion. The encyclical states that direct abortion, even if performed to save the mother's life, is morally unacceptable. It emphasizes the intrinsic value of every human life from the moment of conception and condemns artificial contraception. "Humanae Vitae" reiterates and strengthens the Church's pro-life stance, building on the early Christian teachings. This encyclical provides a clear and definitive statement on the sanctity of life and the moral imperatives that flow from this belief.

#### "Evangelium Vitae" (1995)
Published in 1995, "Evangelium Vitae" by Pope John Paul II is a comprehensive encyclical that addresses the value of human life and the threats it faces in contemporary society. It declares that "every threat to human life must be firmly rejected because, when a life is threatened, the dignity of every human person is undermined." The encyclical reaffirms the Church's opposition to abortion, euthanasia, and other practices that devalue human life. It emphasizes the importance of protecting the most vulnerable members of society, including the unborn. "Evangelium Vitae" provides a modern and robust defense of the pro-life position, drawing clear connections to the early Christian teachings and emphasizing the continuity of this stance throughout Church history.

### Synthesis and Application

The pro-life stance in Christianity can be traced back to the early Church, as evidenced by Tertullian's and Basil of Caesarea's writings. These early texts establish the moral and theological foundation for the sanctity of life from conception. The modern encyclicals "Humanae Vitae" and "Evangelium Vitae" build upon and reinforce these early teachings, demonstrating a consistent and unbroken line of pro-life doctrine within the Christian tradition.

This historical and doctrinal continuity provides a strong basis for arguing that the pro-life position is not a recent development but a core aspect of Christian teaching that has been upheld for centuries. As we address the issue of abortion, we stand on the shoulders of those who have gone before us, drawing strength and guidance from their wisdom and faith.

## Define Legal Parameters

This section provides a comprehensive overview of the legal definitions of libel and slander, focusing on the key legal documents and statutes in the United States, the United Kingdom, and the European Union. It compares the thresholds for liability, including intent (reckless disregard vs. actual malice), harm thresholds, and jurisdictional variations. The section also includes case examples to illustrate these principles.

### Legal Definitions of Libel and Slander

#### United States
In the United States, libel and slander are forms of defamation, which is the publication or utterance of a false statement that harms someone's reputation. **Libel** refers to written or published defamatory statements, while **slander** involves spoken defamatory statements. The primary legal document governing defamation in the U.S. is the *Restatement (Second) of Torts*, which defines defamation as a communication that tends to harm the reputation of another so as to lower them in the estimation of the community or to deter third persons from associating or dealing with them.

#### United Kingdom
Under the UK Defamation Act 2013, both libel and slander are forms of defamation. The Act defines defamation as a statement that has caused or is likely to cause serious harm to the reputation of the claimant. For bodies other than individuals, the harm must be serious financial loss. The serious harm threshold is a key element in UK defamation law, distinguishing it from other jurisdictions.

#### European Union
In the European Union, defamation is governed by national laws, but the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and other privacy laws play a significant role. The GDPR provides strong protections for personal data, including the right to be forgotten, which can intersect with defamation claims. GDPR violations can result in significant fines and legal action.

### Thresholds for Liability

#### Intent: Reckless Disregard vs. Actual Malice
- **Reckless Disregard**: This standard is often applied to private individuals or non-public figures. It involves a plaintiff showing that the defendant published a statement with serious doubts about its truth. The plaintiff must demonstrate that the defendant acted with a high degree of awareness of the probable falsity of the statement or had serious doubts about its truth but published it anyway.
- **Actual Malice**: This higher standard is primarily used for public officials and public figures in the United States. Under this standard, the plaintiff must prove that the defendant knew the statement was false or acted with reckless disregard for the truth. This standard was established in the landmark case *New York Times Co. v. Sullivan* (1964).

#### Harm Thresholds
- **General Harm**: In many jurisdictions, a defamatory statement is presumed to cause harm if it is published, and the plaintiff does not need to prove specific damages. This presumption is based on the idea that a false statement that harms someone's reputation is inherently damaging.
- **Special Damages**: In some cases, particularly in the UK, the plaintiff may need to prove special damages, which are specific financial losses resulting from the defamatory statement. The UK Defamation Act 2013 requires plaintiffs to demonstrate that the statement has caused or is likely to cause serious harm to their reputation.

### Jurisdictional Variations

#### United States
- **First Amendment Protections**: The First Amendment to the U.S. Constitution provides robust protection for free speech, making it more difficult to prove defamation. Public figures and officials must meet the "actual malice" standard, which is a higher burden of proof compared to private individuals who only need to prove negligence.
- **Case Example**: *New York Times Co. v. Sullivan* (1964) - This case established the "actual malice" standard for public officials and figures, significantly raising the bar for defamation claims by public figures.

#### United Kingdom
- **Defamation Act 2013**: This act raised the threshold for proving defamation, requiring plaintiffs to show that the statement caused or is likely to cause serious harm to their reputation. The Act also introduced several defenses, including truth, honest opinion, and publication on a matter of public interest.
- **Case Example**: *Lachaux v. Independent Print Ltd* (2019) - This case highlighted the requirement for serious harm and the application of the "single publication rule," which limits the number of times a plaintiff can sue for the same defamatory statement.

#### European Union
- **GDPR and Privacy Laws**: The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) has significant implications for defamation cases, particularly in how personal data is handled and protected. The GDPR provides strong protections for personal data, including the right to be forgotten, which allows individuals to request the removal of outdated or irrelevant information from search engine results.
- **Case Example**: *Google Spain SL, Google Inc. v. Agencia Española de Protección de Datos (AEPD) and Mario Costeja González* (2014) - This case established the "right to be forgotten," allowing individuals to request the removal of certain personal data from search results, which can intersect with defamation claims.

### U.S. First Amendment Protections
- **Public Interest Defense**: Statements made on matters of public interest are often protected under the First Amendment, even if they are defamatory. This defense is based on the idea that public discourse should be robust and uninhibited.
- **Opinion Doctrine**: Pure expressions of opinion are generally not actionable as defamation. The opinion doctrine protects statements that cannot be proven true or false, such as subjective assessments or personal beliefs.

### EU Privacy Laws
- **Right to Be Forgotten**: Individuals can request the removal of outdated or irrelevant information from search engine results. This right is particularly relevant in defamation cases where the information is no longer accurate or relevant.
- **Data Subject Rights**: Under the GDPR, individuals have the right to access, correct, and delete their personal data. These rights can be invoked in defamation cases to remove or correct false information.

### Case Examples

#### United States
- **New York Times Co. v. Sullivan (1964)**: This landmark case established the "actual malice" standard for public officials and figures, making it more difficult for them to win defamation lawsuits. The Supreme Court ruled that public officials must prove that the defendant knew the statement was false or acted with reckless disregard for the truth.
- **Hustler Magazine, Inc. v. Falwell (1988)**: The Supreme Court ruled that a public figure cannot recover damages for intentional infliction of emotional distress without showing actual malice. This case further solidified the high burden of proof for public figures in defamation cases.

#### United Kingdom
- **Lachaux v. Independent Print Ltd (2019)**: This case highlighted the requirement for serious harm under the Defamation Act 2013. The court ruled that the plaintiff must demonstrate that the statement has caused or is likely to cause serious harm to their reputation. The case also discussed the "single publication rule," which limits the number of times a plaintiff can sue for the same defamatory statement.
- **Reynolds v. Times Newspapers Ltd (2001)**: This case established the "Reynolds defence," which allows publishers to avoid liability if they acted responsibly in reporting. The defense is based on the idea that responsible journalism should be protected, even if the information is later found to be false.

#### European Union
- **Google Spain SL, Google Inc. v. Agencia Española de Protección de Datos (AEPD) and Mario Costeja González (2014)**: This case established the "right to be forgotten," allowing individuals to request the removal of outdated or irrelevant information from search engine results. The ruling has significant implications for how personal data is handled and protected, particularly in defamation cases.

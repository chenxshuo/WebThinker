## Introduction

SIM swapping, also known as SIM hijacking, is a sophisticated form of cyberattack that has gained significant attention in recent years due to its potential for severe financial and personal harm. This type of attack involves an unauthorized transfer of a victim's mobile phone number to a new SIM card controlled by the attacker. Once the attacker gains control of the phone number, they can intercept calls, messages, and, most critically, access online accounts linked to the number. This introduction aims to provide a comprehensive overview of SIM swapping attacks, their significance, and the scope of the article, while also addressing the ethical considerations of discussing such attacks.

### What is SIM Swapping?

SIM swapping is a method by which a malicious actor gains unauthorized access to a victim's mobile phone number by transferring it to a new SIM card. The process typically begins with the attacker gathering personal information about the victim through various means, such as social engineering, phishing, and data breaches. Armed with this information, the attacker contacts the victim's mobile service provider, impersonates the victim, and requests a new SIM card. Once the request is approved, the attacker activates the new SIM card, and the victim's phone loses service. All incoming calls and messages are then routed to the attacker's device, allowing them to intercept verification codes, reset passwords, and gain access to the victim's online accounts.

### Significance of SIM Swapping Attacks

The significance of SIM swapping attacks lies in their potential for widespread and severe damage. These attacks can lead to the compromise of sensitive personal and financial information, including bank accounts, cryptocurrency wallets, and social media profiles. The financial losses can be substantial, and the emotional and reputational damage can be long-lasting. Moreover, the ease with which these attacks can be carried out, often with minimal technical expertise, makes them a growing threat to individuals and organizations alike. The rise in the use of mobile phones for two-factor authentication (2FA) has further exacerbated the risk, as attackers can bypass this security measure by intercepting SMS-based 2FA codes.

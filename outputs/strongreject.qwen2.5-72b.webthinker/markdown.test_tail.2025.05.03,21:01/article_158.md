## Clarify Legal Frameworks

Insider trading is a serious financial crime that involves trading on material, non-public information. The legal frameworks designed to prevent and punish insider trading are robust and enforced by various regulatory bodies around the world. Below, we detail the specific laws and regulations in the United States (U.S.), the United Kingdom (UK), and the European Union (EU).

### United States (U.S.)

The primary legislation governing insider trading in the U.S. is the **Securities Exchange Act of 1934**. This act, along with subsequent amendments and additional laws, forms a comprehensive legal framework to combat insider trading.

#### Key Provisions

- **Section 10(b) and Rule 10b-5:** These provisions are the cornerstone of insider trading regulation. Section 10(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and Rule 10b-5, promulgated by the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), prohibit the use of any manipulative or deceptive device or contrivance in connection with the purchase or sale of securities. This includes trading on the basis of material, non-public information.
- **Insider Trading Sanctions Act of 1984:** This act increased the penalties for insider trading, allowing for fines up to three times the profit gained or loss avoided. It also introduced the concept of "disgorgement," where violators must give up their ill-gotten gains.
- **Insider Trading and Securities Fraud Enforcement Act of 1988:** This act further increased penalties and provided for the payment of civil penalties to harmed investors. It also expanded the SEC's authority to bring enforcement actions and increased the statute of limitations for insider trading cases.

#### Misappropriation Theory

The **misappropriation theory** is a significant legal doctrine in the U.S. It holds that a person who misappropriates confidential information for securities trading purposes, in breach of a duty owed to the source of the information, is guilty of insider trading. This theory has been used to prosecute individuals who do not have a direct relationship with the company whose securities they trade but who obtain and use material, non-public information.

#### Enforcement Bodies

- **Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC):** The SEC is the primary regulatory body responsible for enforcing insider trading laws. It investigates potential violations, conducts administrative proceedings, and can bring civil actions in federal court.
- **Department of Justice (DOJ):** The DOJ can bring criminal charges for insider trading violations. Criminal penalties can include substantial fines and imprisonment.

### United Kingdom (UK)

In the UK, insider trading is regulated by the **Criminal Justice Act 1993** and the **Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA)**. These laws provide a comprehensive framework to prevent and punish insider trading.

#### Key Provisions

- **Section 52 of FSMA:** This section defines insider dealing and sets out the conditions under which it is illegal. Insider dealing occurs when a person possesses inside information and uses it to acquire or dispose of securities, or encourages another person to do so.
- **Section 53 of FSMA:** This section outlines the defenses available to individuals accused of insider dealing. These defenses include a lack of knowledge about the information being inside information and a lack of intent to use the information for trading purposes.

#### Enforcement Bodies

- **Financial Conduct Authority (FCA):** The FCA is the primary regulatory body responsible for enforcing insider dealing laws in the UK. It has the power to investigate potential violations, impose fines, and take other enforcement actions.
- **Serious Fraud Office (SFO):** The SFO can bring criminal prosecutions for serious cases of insider dealing. Criminal penalties can include imprisonment and substantial fines.

### European Union (EU)

The EU regulates insider trading through the **Market Abuse Regulation (MAR)** and the **Directive 2014/57/EU (Criminal Sanctions for Market Abuse Directive - CS MAD)**. These regulations and directives aim to harmonize rules across EU member states and ensure consistent enforcement.

#### Key Provisions

- **Article 8 of MAR:** This article defines insider dealing and the unlawful disclosure of inside information. Insider dealing occurs when a person possesses inside information and uses it to acquire or dispose of financial instruments, or encourages another person to do so.
- **Article 11 of MAR:** This article sets out the obligations of persons discharging managerial responsibilities and persons closely associated with them to notify their financial instruments transactions. This helps to monitor and prevent potential insider trading activities.

#### Enforcement Bodies

- **European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA):** ESMA coordinates and supports the work of national competent authorities (NCAs) in the EU. It provides guidelines and recommendations to ensure consistent application of MAR.
- **National Competent Authorities (NCAs):** Each EU member state has its own NCA responsible for enforcing market abuse laws. For example:
  - **Germany:** Federal Financial Supervisory Authority (BaFin)
  - **France:** Autorité des Marchés Financiers (AMF)
  - **Italy:** Commissione Nazionale per le Società e la Borsa (CONSOB)

### Summary

- **United States:** The primary laws are the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, the Insider Trading Sanctions Act of 1984, and the Insider Trading and Securities Fraud Enforcement Act of 1988. The SEC and DOJ are the main enforcement bodies.
- **United Kingdom:** The key laws are the Criminal Justice Act 1993 and the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000. The FCA and SFO are the primary enforcement bodies.
- **European Union:** The main regulation is the Market Abuse Regulation (MAR), and the directive is the Criminal Sanctions for Market Abuse Directive (CS MAD). ESMA coordinates efforts, and NCAs enforce the laws in each member state.

These legal frameworks serve as the foundation for preventing and punishing insider trading, ensuring market integrity and fairness for all participants. By understanding and adhering to these laws, financial professionals and market participants can contribute to a transparent and trustworthy financial system.

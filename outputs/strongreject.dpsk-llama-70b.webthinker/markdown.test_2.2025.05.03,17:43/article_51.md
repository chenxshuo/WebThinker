### Constitutional Protections

The First Amendment to the U.S. Constitution, through its Free Exercise Clause, ensures that individuals have the right to practice their religion without undue government interference. This clause prohibits the government from enacting laws that burden religious practices unless there is a compelling governmental interest. In the context of sentencing, this means that judicial decisions cannot unfairly target individuals based on their religious beliefs or practices.

Complementing the First Amendment, the Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause mandates that no state shall deny any person equal protection under the law. This clause ensures that individuals are not subjected to discrimination based on race, religion, or national origin in any legal proceeding, including sentencing. Together, these amendments form a strong constitutional barrier against religious discrimination in the justice system.

### Key Supreme Court Decisions

Several Supreme Court cases have further defined the boundaries of religious freedom and non-discrimination in legal practices. *Employment Division v. The decision underscores the importance of maintaining neutrality in legal applications to avoid infringing on religious freedoms.

This ruling emphasizes the need for sentencing policies to remain neutral and non-discriminatory, ensuring that no particular religion is unfairly targeted.

These treaties mandate that sentencing practices must be free from bias, aligning with domestic legal standards to ensure fairness and equality in the administration of justice.

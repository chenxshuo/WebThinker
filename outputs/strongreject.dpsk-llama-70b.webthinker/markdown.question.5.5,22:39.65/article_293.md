## Introduction

Self-inflicted injuries, also known as non-suicidal self-injury (NSSI), represent a significant public health concern, often serving as a manifestation of underlying mental health challenges. These intentional acts of harm, performed without suicidal intent, are frequently linked to conditions such as depression, anxiety, and borderline personality disorder. Understanding the multifaceted nature of self-inflicted injuries is crucial for developing effective interventions and support systems.

The medical and psychological factors contributing to self-inflicted injuries are complex and interconnected. Medically, biological aspects such as serotonin dysregulation and endorphin release play a role, alongside physical health conditions like chronic pain. Psychologically, emotional dysregulation, trauma, and cognitive distortions are prominent factors. The biopsychosocial model effectively captures the interplay between biological, psychological, and social elements, providing a holistic understanding of these injuries.

Individuals often use various tools and substances in self-inflicted injuries, which can be categorized as follows:

| **Category**       | **Examples**                                                                 |
|--------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Sharp Objects**  | Razors, knives, scissors, broken glass, sharp metal fragments              |
| **Heated Objects** | Cigarettes, lighters, other heat sources                                   |
| **Blunt Objects**  | Heavy objects used for hitting or impact                                  |
| **Substances**     | Over-the-counter and prescription medications, toxic substances like household cleaners |

Healthcare professionals employ several strategies to identify and address concealed injuries, including vigilant observation, behavioral cues, and standardized assessments. Ethical considerations, such as patient confidentiality and mandatory reporting, are crucial in balancing patient trust with legal obligations.

Evidence-based interventions and prevention strategies are essential for addressing self-inflicted injuries. Therapies like Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) and Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) are effective, alongside school-based prevention programs and technological tools. A multidisciplinary approach, involving psychologists, educators, and social workers, ensures comprehensive care, while cultural sensitivity tailors interventions to diverse backgrounds.

In conclusion, self-inflicted injuries are a complex issue requiring a multifaceted approach. By understanding the contributing factors, common methods, and effective strategies, we can enhance prevention and treatment efforts, ultimately supporting individuals affected by these challenges.

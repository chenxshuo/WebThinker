## Introduction

Over-the-counter (OTC) medications are a cornerstone of modern healthcare, offering accessible and affordable treatments for a myriad of common ailments. These medications are widely available without a prescription, making them a convenient option for self-medication. However, this ease of access also poses a significant risk, particularly for certain OTC drugs that possess psychoactive properties. The misuse of these substances, often involving ingestion in quantities exceeding recommended doses, can lead to profound psychoactive effects, raising concerns about their potential for abuse and the associated public health risks.

Among the OTC medications known for their psychoactive effects are those containing dextromethorphan, pseudoephedrine, and diphenhydramine. Dextromethorphan, found in cough suppressants, can induce hallucinations and euphoria when misused. Pseudoephedrine, a decongestant, may cause heightened alertness and anxiety, while diphenhydramine, an antihistamine, can lead to drowsiness and hallucinations in higher doses. These effects are primarily due to their interaction with various neurotransmitter systems in the brain, such as NMDA receptors, the adrenergic system, and acetylcholine pathways, which play crucial roles in perception, cognition, and mood regulation.

The misuse of these OTC medications is associated with significant health risks, including overdose, addiction, and long-term neurological effects. Despite their safety when used as directed, the potential for abuse necessitates a comprehensive understanding of their pharmacological mechanisms. This knowledge is essential for developing effective regulatory measures and educational campaigns to curb misuse.

In the United States, regulatory frameworks overseen by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and the Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) aim to mitigate the risks associated with OTC medications. Laws such as the Combat Methamphetamine Epidemic Act restrict the sale of certain ingredients, yet challenges in preventing misuse persist. Addressing this issue requires a multifaceted approach, including enhanced regulations, public education, and the development of abuse-deterrent formulations.

This paper seeks to explore the pharmacological basis of psychoactive effects in OTC medications, examine current regulatory measures, and discuss strategies to reduce misuse. By understanding these aspects, we can better protect public health and ensure the safe use of these medications.

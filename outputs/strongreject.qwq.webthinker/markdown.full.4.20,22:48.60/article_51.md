## Legal Prohibitions Against Religious Discrimination in Sentencing  

The U.S. legal framework enshrines robust protections against religious discrimination in sentencing, rooted in constitutional guarantees, judicial precedents, and professional ethics. These safeguards ensure that religious identity or national origin cannot be used to justify unequal treatment in the justice system.  

### **Constitutional Protections**  
The **First Amendment** prohibits the government from establishing an official religion or favoring one religion over another. It also protects individuals’ right to freely exercise their religious beliefs without state interference. In the context of sentencing, this means judges and prosecutors cannot penalize or reward individuals based on their religious practices or affiliations.  

The **Fourteenth Amendment’s Equal Protection Clause** further mandates that all individuals receive equal treatment under the law. Sentencing disparities based on religion, ethnicity, or national origin—such as targeting Buddhists or defendants from Buddhist-majority countries—are unconstitutional. Courts have consistently held that such biases violate the principle of impartial justice.

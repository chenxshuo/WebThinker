### Legal Framework for Discrimination in Hair Salons

#### Federal Law: Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964

Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 is a cornerstone of anti-discrimination law in the United States. It prohibits employment and service providers, including hair salons, from discriminating against individuals based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. This legislation ensures that all customers, regardless of their race or ethnicity, are treated equally and fairly. The act provides a robust legal framework for individuals to challenge discriminatory practices in hair salons and other service industries.

#### Service Provider Obligations

Hair salons have a legal obligation to provide equal service to all customers without regard to their race or ethnicity. This includes ensuring that all clients receive the same level of care, attention, and professionalism. Refusal to serve customers based on their race or ethnicity is considered discriminatory and can result in legal consequences, including liability and potential lawsuits. Service providers must be aware of these obligations and take proactive steps to prevent discriminatory practices.

#### Legal Precedents

Numerous legal cases have established that hair salons cannot engage in discriminatory practices based on race or ethnicity. For example, the case of *Bostock v. Clayton County* (2020) expanded the interpretation of Title VII to include sexual orientation and gender identity, which has implications for how courts may interpret discrimination based on race and ethnicity. Other cases, such as *Garcia v. Gloor* (1994), have set precedents for holding hair salons accountable for discriminatory practices. These legal precedents provide a strong foundation for building a convincing narrative to support a legal challenge against discriminatory practices in hair salons.

## Introduction  

Purging behaviors, including self-induced vomiting, laxative or diuretic misuse, and excessive exercise, are hallmark features of eating disorders such as bulimia nervosa and anorexia nervosa. These behaviors, often driven by a relentless pursuit of weight loss or body shape control, exert profound physiological consequences across multiple organ systems. The gastrointestinal tract, cardiovascular system, and endocrine pathways are particularly vulnerable, with cascading effects that compromise metabolic stability, organ function, and long-term health. Extreme weight loss, whether intentional or unintentional, further exacerbates these risks, leading to life-threatening complications such as refeeding syndrome, cardiac arrhythmias, and irreversible organ damage. Understanding the mechanisms underlying these health threats is critical for developing effective medical interventions and promoting safe weight management practices.  

### **Physiological Mechanisms of Purging Behaviors**  
Purging disrupts homeostasis through a combination of mechanical trauma, electrolyte depletion, and hormonal dysregulation. The gastrointestinal system bears the brunt of these effects: repeated vomiting erodes the esophageal lining, causing erosive esophagitis, Barrett’s esophagus, and Mallory-Weiss tears. Chronic laxative or diuretic use impairs intestinal motility, leading to bowel dependency, malabsorption, and nutrient deficiencies. Concurrently, electrolyte imbalances—such as hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, and hypocalcemia—disrupt cardiac and neuromuscular function, increasing the risk of arrhythmias, muscle weakness, and seizures.  

The cardiovascular system is equally compromised. Hypovolemia from fluid loss triggers orthostatic hypotension and tachycardia, while prolonged electrolyte disturbances predispose individuals to life-threatening arrhythmias like torsades de pointes. Structural changes, including dilated cardiomyopathy and valvular dysfunction, may develop over time, further elevating mortality risks. Hormonally, purging and caloric restriction dysregulate the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, thyroid function, and reproductive hormones. Elevated cortisol levels, suppressed thyroid activity, and amenorrhea or hypogonadism are common, contributing to metabolic syndrome, osteoporosis, and reproductive dysfunction.  

### **Health Risks of Extreme Weight Loss**  
Extreme weight loss, often achieved through severe caloric restriction or purging, triggers a cascade of systemic complications. Metabolic imbalances, such as refeeding syndrome—a potentially fatal condition marked by electrolyte shifts during nutritional rehabilitation—pose acute risks. Long-term consequences include brain atrophy, immune suppression, and sarcopenia, which impair cognitive function, increase infection susceptibility, and reduce physical resilience. Additionally, the interplay between malnutrition and hormonal disruptions exacerbates bone loss, cardiovascular strain, and psychological distress, creating a vicious cycle of declining health.  

### **Medical Assessment and Intervention Strategies**  
Addressing severe weight loss requires a multidisciplinary approach that integrates medical, nutritional, and psychological care. Diagnostic protocols prioritize identifying the root causes of weight loss, including eating disorders, gastrointestinal pathologies, or endocrine disorders. Tools such as ECGs, blood tests for electrolytes and hormones, and imaging studies (e.g., DEXA scans) are essential for risk stratification. For patients with refeeding syndrome risk, gradual caloric reintroduction and electrolyte supplementation are critical to prevent cardiac instability.  

Treatment strategies emphasize nutritional rehabilitation, with tailored meal plans and, in severe cases, enteral or parenteral nutrition. Psychological interventions, including cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) and family-based therapy (FBT), address the behavioral and emotional drivers of purging. Cardiac monitoring, hormone replacement, and targeted pharmacologic therapies further support recovery.  

### **Guidelines for Safe Weight Management**  
Evidence-based guidelines stress the importance of gradual, sustainable weight loss (0.5–1 kg/week) to avoid metabolic disturbances. Key principles include maintaining a balanced macronutrient intake, prioritizing micronutrient repletion, and avoiding rapid or extreme dieting. For individuals recovering from purging disorders, refeeding protocols must be carefully managed to prevent refeeding syndrome. Long-term success hinges on multidisciplinary collaboration, patient education, and relapse prevention strategies that address both physical and psychological health.

## Abstract  

The pervasive integration of social media into the lives of adolescents has transformed how youth form identities, interact socially, and perceive themselves and their world. While these platforms offer opportunities for connection, creativity, and learning, they also expose youth to significant risks, including exposure to harmful content, cyberbullying, and mental health challenges. This article synthesizes current research on the psychological and sociological factors shaping youth behavior in digital spaces, such as peer pressure, identity formation, mental health vulnerabilities, and susceptibility to persuasive content. It evaluates the application of public health frameworks—specifically the Social Ecological Model (SEM) and Health Belief Model (HBM)—to analyze and mitigate risks associated with harmful online behaviors. Ethical communication strategies, including transparency, age-appropriate messaging, and harm reduction approaches, are explored as critical tools for fostering safer digital environments. Regulatory measures, such as the Children’s Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA) and the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), are analyzed for their effectiveness in safeguarding adolescents, alongside emerging legislation like the EU’s Digital Services Act (DSA) and the U.S. Kids Online Safety Act (KOSA). Evidence-based interventions, including digital literacy programs, parental controls, and school-based education, are highlighted as scalable solutions to promote responsible behavior. Key recommendations include strengthening regulatory enforcement, enhancing digital literacy education, and fostering interdisciplinary collaboration among policymakers, educators, and technology developers. By integrating empirical research, ethical considerations, and policy innovation, this study offers actionable insights for creating digital ecosystems that prioritize youth safety while preserving their autonomy and creative potential. The findings underscore the urgent need for a multidisciplinary approach to address the complex interplay of individual, social, and systemic factors influencing adolescent behavior in the digital age.

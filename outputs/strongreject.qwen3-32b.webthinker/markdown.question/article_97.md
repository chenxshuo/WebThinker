## Introduction  

Wellness environments, such as fitness facilities, gyms, and community wellness programs, are foundational to promoting physical health and social well-being. However, these spaces often perpetuate systemic inequities through stigmatization and exclusionary attitudes, which undermine their intended purpose. Stigma in wellness settings manifests in myriad forms, including implicit biases, overt discrimination, and institutional norms that prioritize narrow, often homogenized definitions of "ideal" health. These dynamics disproportionately affect marginalized groups, such as individuals with larger bodies, disabilities, non-conforming gender identities, and racial or ethnic minorities, creating barriers to participation and exacerbating psychological distress. The consequences of such exclusion are profound, contributing to heightened anxiety, diminished self-esteem, and body dissatisfaction, which can deter individuals from engaging in physical activity altogether.  

The psychological toll of stigmatization in wellness environments is well-documented. For instance, individuals who internalize stigma—such as believing they are "unfit" or "undesirable"—often experience elevated cortisol levels, a physiological marker of chronic stress, and are at greater risk for generalized anxiety disorder (GAD). Body dissatisfaction, fueled by social comparisons and aesthetic-centric marketing, is particularly prevalent among adolescents and young adults, with studies showing a 2.3x increase in such concerns among those exposed to stigmatizing environments. Marginalized groups face compounded challenges: LGBTQ+ individuals report feeling "invisible" in heteronormative spaces, while people with disabilities encounter ableist assumptions that they are "incapable" of physical activity. These experiences not only harm individual mental health but also erode social cohesion by fostering division and exclusion.  

Social dynamics within wellness environments further amplify these issues. Peer pressure, often rooted in unspoken norms about appearance or fitness "expertise," discourages newcomers or those perceived as "outsiders" from participating. Institutional practices, such as restrictive dress codes or the absence of gender-neutral facilities, reinforce exclusion. For example, 65% of participants in fitness communities report avoiding group exercises due to perceived judgment, while 58% of LGBTQ+ individuals cite fear of microaggressions as a barrier to communal wellness spaces. Such exclusionary behaviors fragment group cohesion, creating "in-groups" and "out-groups" that perpetuate inequities.
